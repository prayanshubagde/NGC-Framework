## Introduction

The Leibniz-Bocker Framework is the operational calculus for measuring and analyzing dynamical systems through the lens of geometric computation. It provides the mathematical tools to decompose motion into structure and novelty, and to diagnose phase transitions in real-time.

This framework is named in honor of Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, whose vision of a *characteristica universalis* (a universal language of reasoning based on geometry) laid the philosophical foundation for this work, and Nathanael J. Bocker, who formalized the mathematical implementation.

## Historical Context

### Leibniz's Vision (1666)

In his *Dissertatio de arte combinatoria* (1666), Leibniz proposed that all human reasoning could be reduced to geometric relationships and combinatorial operations. He envisioned a system where:

1. **Concepts are represented geometrically**
2. **Reasoning is performed through geometric transformations**
3. **Truth is determined by structural coherence**

Leibniz's insight was that **form precedes number**-that the relationships between entities are more fundamental than the entities themselves.

### The Leibniz-Newton Rivalry

The famous rivalry between Leibniz and Newton over the invention of calculus was not merely a priority dispute. It was a fundamental disagreement about the nature of change:

- **Newton's approach:** Change is absolute, measured against an external reference frame (absolute space and time).
- **Leibniz's approach:** Change is relational, measured by the relationships between entities (relational space and time).

Modern physics has vindicated Leibniz. Einstein's relativity is fundamentally Leibnizian-there is no absolute reference frame, only relationships.

The Leibniz-Bocker Framework extends this insight to computation itself.

## Core Principle: Infinitesimal Motion as Primary Data

The Leibniz-Bocker Framework treats **infinitesimal motion** as the fundamental unit of analysis:

**φ(t) = x(t+1) - x(t)**

This is not the state of the system. This is **how the system is trying to move**. Leibniz's insight was that systems are best understood not by where they are, but by where they're going.

## The Three Diagnostics

The framework decomposes system dynamics into three measurable quantities:

### 1. Coherence (ρₖ(t))

**Definition:** The fraction of variance captured by the dominant k modes.

**Formula:**  
ρₖ(t) = Σᵢ₌₁ᵏ σᵢ² / Σⱼ₌₁ᴺ σⱼ²

Where σᵢ are the singular values from the SVD of the motion matrix.

**Physical Meaning:** How much of the system's behavior can be explained by a low-dimensional structure?

- **High coherence (ρ → 1):** The system is highly structured, predictable, in the IR phase.
- **Low coherence (ρ → 0):** The system is chaotic, unpredictable, in the UV phase.

**Threshold:** ε_threshold = √2 / 21 ≈ 0.06734

When ρₖ(t) < ε_threshold, the system is in a phase transition.

### 2. Curvature (Ωₖ(t))

**Definition:** The rate of subspace rotation, measuring how quickly the dominant modes are changing.

**Formula:**  
Ωₖ(t) = ||Uₖ(t)ᵀ Uₖ(t-1) - I||_F

Where Uₖ(t) is the matrix of the top k left singular vectors at time t.

**Physical Meaning:** How fast is the system's structure evolving?

- **Low curvature (Ω → 0):** The system's structure is stable.
- **High curvature (Ω → ∞):** The system's structure is rapidly changing (phase transition).

### 3. Residual Energy (||r(t)||²)

**Definition:** The energy in the motion that is orthogonal to the dominant structure.

**Formula:**  
||r(t)||² = Σⱼ₌ₖ₊₁ᴺ σⱼ²

**Physical Meaning:** How much novelty or "surprise" is the system experiencing?

- **Low residual (||r|| → 0):** The system is fully explained by its current structure.
- **High residual (||r|| → ∞):** The system is encountering new information that doesn't fit the current structure.

## The Grassmann Manifold

The Leibniz-Bocker Framework operates on the **Grassmann manifold** Gr(k, N)-the space of all k-dimensional subspaces of ℝᴺ.

Each point on this manifold represents a possible "structure" or "mode" of the system. As the system evolves, it traces a trajectory through this space.

The three diagnostics (coherence, curvature, residual) provide a complete description of this trajectory:

- **Coherence:** How concentrated the system is on a single point (subspace)
- **Curvature:** How fast the system is moving through the manifold
- **Residual:** How much energy is pushing the system off its current trajectory
